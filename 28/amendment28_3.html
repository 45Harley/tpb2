Amendment 28

The National Ballot Initiative
26 states let citizens propose laws directly.
The federal government does not.
It's time to change that.

The Problem

Congress has a 17% approval rating. Yet Congress is the only way to change federal law.

Americans can vote for representatives, but we cannot vote on issues directly. We have no mechanism to:

  • Propose term limits (Congress won't limit itself)
  • Reform campaign finance (Congress won't cut its own funding)
  • Fix the Electoral College (the parties benefit from it)
  • Address any issue Congress refuses to touch

The Founders gave us a representative democracy. But they also said:

"The people were in fact, the fountain of all power, and by resorting to them, all difficulties were got over. They could alter constitutions as they pleased." — James Madison, Constitutional Convention, 1787

The Solution: Amendment 28

Create a National Ballot Initiative — giving citizens the power to propose and pass federal laws directly, bypassing Congress when necessary.

Proposed Text

Section 1. The People of the United States shall have the power to propose and enact laws and constitutional amendments through national ballot initiative.

Section 2. An initiative shall appear on the national ballot upon petition by registered voters equal to five percent of votes cast in the preceding presidential election, collected from at least three-fourths of the states.

Section 3. A proposed law shall become law upon approval by a majority of voters in a majority of states. A proposed constitutional amendment shall become part of this Constitution upon approval by a majority of voters in three-fourths of the states.

Section 4. Congress shall have power to enforce this article by appropriate legislation, including provisions for petition verification, ballot access, and election administration.

How It Would Work

Now (Article V)

To amend the Constitution:

  1. 2/3 of House votes yes
  2. 2/3 of Senate votes yes
  3. 3/4 of state legislatures ratify

Congress controls the gate.

With Amendment 28

Alternative path:

  1. Citizens petition (5% in 38 states)
  2. Issue goes on national ballot
  3. Majority in 3/4 of states = passes

The People control the gate.

The History of Trying

Americans have fought for this power for over a century:

1907
Rep. Elmer Fulton (D-OK) introduces H.R. 44 — the first national initiative proposal. Failed.
1977
After Vietnam and Watergate, Senators James Abourezk (D-SD) and Mark Hatfield (R-OR) introduce S.J.R. 67 — the National Voter Initiative. Rep. Guy Vander Jagt (R-MI) introduces H.J.R. 544 in the House. Both failed.
2003
Former Senator Mike Gravel (D-AK) launches the National Initiative for Democracy (ni4d.us), attempting to have citizens ratify the initiative directly without Congress. The effort stalled after Gravel's death in 2021.
2025
The People's Branch relaunches the effort with modern infrastructure. This time, we don't stop.

We the People Demand That Congress Enact Amendment 28

Every previous attempt failed for the same reason: Congress must vote to limit its own power.

That's like asking a king to abolish the monarchy.

But the Constitution provides another path. Article V allows 2/3 of state legislatures to call a Constitutional Convention. And Amendment 28, once passed, creates a permanent path for future amendments that bypasses Congress entirely.

One amendment unlocks all future amendments.

Common Objections

"The Founders rejected direct democracy"

They rejected pure direct democracy. They created a republic with democratic elements. Twenty-six states have added ballot initiatives since then — and remain republics. This isn't replacing representative government; it's adding a safety valve when representatives fail.

"People will vote for stupid things"

People already vote for representatives who do stupid things. At least with a ballot initiative, citizens have to read the actual proposal — not just a party label. The 3/4 state supermajority requirement for amendments ensures broad consensus.

"Special interests will buy signatures"

Special interests already buy representatives. The difference: you can't bribe 150 million voters. A national initiative actually makes corruption harder, not easier.

"This is unconstitutional"

Constitutional amendments can't be unconstitutional — they become the Constitution. The 17th Amendment changed how we elect senators. The 19th gave women the vote. The 22nd limited presidential terms. Each changed the system. That's what amendments do.
